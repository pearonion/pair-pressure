VERY NORMAL HUMAN SIMULATOR
Brand-New 3+ Player MVP Game Design Document
UE5.8 Implementation-Focused Version 2
One-Sentence PitchOne player is the Hunter, one player is secretly an Alien operating a human body, and the remaining players are Human civilians creating believable chaos. The Hunter uses public commands, one direct question, and one accusation to identify who is pretending.
Design North StarChase fun, memories, laughs, trolling opportunities, deception, and instant rematches. Keep every feature in service of: Watch. React. Lie. Accuse. Reveal.
Document goal: give enough design and technical detail to start coding in Unreal Engine 5.8 while keeping the MVP small enough for a 2-3 person team.

2. Why This Version Should Be More Fun
The previous design risked becoming a checklist stealth game. This version puts people at the centre. Human players create false positives, the Alien hides inside social chaos, and the Hunter creates public situations that force everyone to perform.
Friends are the chaos engine. Human players can be nervous, funny, misleading, or accidentally more suspicious than the Alien.
The Alien is not doing chores. The Alien is trying to survive human rituals and public pressure.
The Hunter is not scanning for a model difference. The Hunter is creating situations and reading reactions.
Every round should produce a story: someone froze weirdly, someone lied badly, the wrong person got accused, or the Alien was in plain sight.
The game is simple to explain: one person is pretending, everyone else is trying not to get blamed.

Recommended: 4 players. One Hunter, one Alien, two Humans.
Maximum MVP: 6 players. One Hunter, one Alien, remaining players are Humans.
Only one Hunter is allowed per round.
Only one Alien is allowed per round.
Roles are randomly assigned every round after the host starts the game.
The same player can receive the same role again, but an optional anti-repeat rule should be added after core testing if players dislike repeats.
Role Randomization GuardrailFor the first implementation, use pure random with exactly one Hunter and one Alien. After playtests, add a soft anti-repeat option: avoid assigning the same player Hunter or Alien three rounds in a row unless player count forces it.

5.2 Lobby Level Flow
The lobby is a small playable level, not just a menu. This helps the game feel social before the round starts.
1. Player chooses Host Public, Host Private, or Join Game.
2. Host loads into the Lobby Level.
3. Friends or public players join the lobby.
4. Players can walk around, emote, test movement, and see who is connected.
5. A large physical Play button or gate exists in the lobby level.
6. Only the host can activate the Play button.
7. When the host runs to the Play button and activates it, the server locks the lobby and begins round loading.
8. The game transitions to a private Role Reveal screen for each player.
9. Each player sees only their own assigned role: Hunter, Alien, or Human.
10. After a short countdown, the Store Level loads and the round starts.

9. Core Mechanics
9.1 Human Body Operation
The central skill is not a task list. It is operating a human body convincingly. All non-Hunter players use the same movement and interaction system so the Hunter must read timing, context, and reaction rather than obvious role-specific powers.
Movement uses acceleration and deceleration rather than instant start/stop.
Body rotation follows movement direction with a subtle turn arc.
Camera can look independently without instantly snapping the body.
Sudden changes cause visible correction steps, but should never feel random.
Standing still is not automatically suspicious if the player uses a believable context: phone, rack, line, mirror, conversation.
Props create readable behaviour: coffee sip, phone check, shopping bag adjust, clothing inspect.
9.2 Public Commands
Public Commands are the Hunter’s main active mechanic. They create normal social situations and let the Hunter watch reactions.
Command
MVP Status
Expected NPC Reaction
Player Challenge
Freeze
Required
NPCs stop in natural poses after short reaction time.
Players must stop smoothly without obvious correction.
Look Here
Required
NPCs glance toward Hunter or announcement point, with role-based variation.
Players must look naturally, not too late, too fast, or too perfectly.

Command Balance RuleCommands should expose reaction quality, not instantly reveal the Alien. Humans can fail too. NPCs can be quirky. The Hunter must connect multiple signs before accusing.

9.5 Suspicious Civilian
Every round should contain at least one suspicious but innocent element. This may be a Human player, an NPC quirk, or both.
One NPC might walk stiffly, avoid the Hunter, hold a bag awkwardly, or repeatedly change their mind.
This prevents the Hunter from simply picking the weirdest character.
The Alien can use suspicious civilians as cover.
Do not make suspicious civilians too extreme in public mode or deduction becomes random.

11. Hunter Design: Preventing Helplessness
Another major worry is that the Hunter may feel bullied by a lobby full of trolls. The Hunter must have strong but limited tools.
Freeze and Look Here create readable group reactions.
The direct question lets the Hunter test one person deeply.
Suspect marking helps the Hunter remember who looked off.
The one accusation creates tension and prevents spam.
Undercover etiquette stops the Hunter from sprinting and harassing everyone, but should not be a hard failure condition in MVP.
Hunter Feel TargetThe Hunter should feel like a clever instigator: “I made everyone react, and now I know who is lying.”
12. Alien Design: Preventing Passivity
The Alien should not win by standing still in the corner. Public Commands, the presence of Human players, and the need to look socially appropriate force the Alien to participate.
No secret task list in MVP. Survival and performance are enough.
Alien can use Act Natural and quick chat like Humans.
Alien receives private alien-thought barks for tension and comedy.
Hunter commands force the Alien to react under pressure.
The Alien should be able to lie through behaviour, not through complex abilities.

18. Networking and Replication
The server must be authoritative over role assignment, commands, direct questions, accusation, round state, and NPC routines. Clients may predict/cosmetically play local animations, but gameplay decisions should validate on server.
System
Replication Rule
Roles
Server assigns. Role is revealed privately to owning client and stored server-side. Other clients only know role at reveal.
Round phase
Replicated through GameState.
Timer
Replicated GameState timer or server timestamp.
Public commands
Server validates Hunter has charges, replicates command event to all clients.
Direct question
Server validates target and charge, sends prompt to target client, replicates visible interaction state.
Accusation
Server validates one accusation, determines result, replicates reveal.
NPCs
Server controls routine selection and Smart Object reservations. Clients receive movement/activity replication.
Quick chat
Server relays line if allowed and cooldown valid.
Act Natural
Client requests, server validates context, multicast cosmetic montage if needed.
MVP Networking RecommendationStart with listen-server sessions. Build all authority boundaries correctly so a dedicated server can be added later without rewriting the whole game.

22. Public Server Guardrails
Public lobbies create additional risk. Build private first, but design public rules early so you do not paint yourself into a corner.
Concern
Guardrail
Humans grief Hunter constantly
Reward believable play and light errands. Track scene disruption for awards/rewards. Keep rounds short.
Hunter harasses everyone
Limit direct question to one, accusation to one, commands to two. NPCs react to aggressive Hunter movement.
Players leave when not Alien/Hunter
Short rounds, role randomization, Human awards, immediate rematch.
Voice toxicity
No public open VOIP in MVP. Use quick chat. Add mute/report before VOIP.
Host abuse
Public lobbies need kick controls, but post-match migration/dedicated servers are post-MVP.
Random guessing
One accusation only. Reveal should show missed clues and awards, not just win/loss.
Too much chaos
Limit Humans’ disruptive tools. Keep public commands server-paced.
23. Major Risks and Mitigations
Risk
Why It Matters
Mitigation
Humans have nothing to do
They become bait and may disengage.
Give light errands, Act Natural, quick chat, awards, and false-positive agency.
Humans troll too hard
Deduction becomes random and Hunter feels bullied.
Reward believable play in public mode; keep trolling social, not mechanical.
Alien becomes passive
Hiding still is boring.

Hunter feels helpless
Everyone lies and Hunter cannot parse clues.

NPCs look too robotic
Hunter cannot tell normal from player-controlled.

Animation quality breaks the game

Public servers become toxic
Social games attract griefing.
Private first. Quick-chat MVP. Add mute/report before VOIP.
3 players is less funny than 4
Only one Human gives fewer false positives.
Support 3, but design/playtest around 4 as ideal.
Scope creeps back up
The old GDD had too many systems.
Keep the scope lock. Any feature must support Watch, React, Lie, Accuse, Reveal.
Role assignment feels unfair
Players may not get desired role.
Short rounds and soft anti-repeat option after core fun is proven.
Hunter wins by model differences
Role becomes visually obvious.
Use identical playable body pipeline for Human and Alien. No role-specific cosmetics in round.
Accusation feels random
Hunter cannot explain decision.
Commands and direct question must create readable evidence. Reveal should show funny clues.

29. Final MVP Statement
Build This FirstA 3+ player social deception round where one Hunter uses Freeze, Look Here, one Direct Question, and one Accusation to find the secretly Alien player among Human players, NPCs, and public chaos inside one clothing store.
If this loop makes players laugh, accuse the wrong person, demand a rematch, and quote the reveal afterward, the game has a foundation. If not, do not add content. Improve the public commands, the social reactions, the movement feel, and the reveal.

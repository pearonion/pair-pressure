1. Executive Summary
Very Normal Human Simulator is a small-scope social deception party-stealth game built around one clear joke: one player is secretly operating a human body and trying not to look like a rented person.
The MVP moves away from large task lists, complex disguise management, and multi-zone stealth. Instead, the game focuses on public social pressure, player-created chaos, and one meaningful accusation.
MVP Rule

Main location

2. Design North Star
This version chases fun first. The game should produce moments that players immediately quote after the round. The target is not a deep stealth simulation. The target is short, hilarious suspicion.
Design Question
Answer
What is the game?
A social hide-and-seek game where one player secretly controls a human body among human players and NPCs.
What is the joke?
The alien looks human but has to perform normality under pressure.
What creates memories?
Wrong accusations, human players acting more alien than the alien, public commands, panic recovery, and confident lies.
What should be cut?
Complex task lists, disguise durability, scanners that solve the game, large crowds, large maps, and heavy progression.
What should be protected?
Simple rules, short rounds, immediate rematch, role secrecy, and player expression.
Player-Facing Pitch
One Sentence
For Alien
Pretend you are a normal customer while the hunter tries to expose you.
For Hunter
Create public situations, watch who reacts wrong, and make one accusation.
For Humans
Act normal, bait suspicion, protect yourself, and laugh when the hunter blames the wrong person.
3. Game Pillars
Pillar
What It Means
Implementation Rule
1. One Simple Lie
One player is the alien. Nobody knows who.
Exactly one Alien and one Hunter per round. All others are Humans.
2. Everyone Performs
Humans, alien, and hunter all roleplay normality.
All civilians share the same controls and actions. Hunter also avoids acting too aggressive.
3. Tests, Not Scanners
The hunter creates situations instead of pressing win buttons.
Public Commands and one Direct Question replace complex scanner tools.
4. Mistakes Become Stories
Errors should be funny, recoverable, and suspicious but not instantly fatal.
Act Natural and contextual mistake recovery are core civilian actions.
5. Fast Rematch
The fun comes from lots of quick rounds, not one long match.
2-3 minute rounds, 10 second reveal, immediate role reroll.
6. Small Map, Big Drama
One store should create enough chaos.
Limit MVP to one store with reusable Smart Objects and 4-8 NPCs.

5. Game Flow: Lobby to Reveal to Store
The requested flow is central to the MVP. The game starts as a social lobby where friends can join, move around, and prepare. The host begins the match by physically running to a Play button inside the lobby level.
5.1 Full Session Flow
Host creates a private or public server.
Players join the lobby level.
Players can walk around, test movement, chat/quick-chat, and see who is connected.
Host runs to the Play button in the lobby and interacts with it.
Server validates minimum 3 players.
Server transitions to Role Assignment.
Each player sees a private role reveal screen: Hunter, Alien, or Human.
Server loads or transitions everyone into the store round.
Round begins after a short countdown.
Hunter uses commands/tests and makes one accusation.
Reveal screen shows everyone’s role and the funniest/worst suspicion moments.
Players return to lobby or immediately rematch with rerolled roles.

6.1 Why Human Players Matter
Human players are the memory engine. They create natural false positives, deliberate trolling, defensive lying, and social uncertainty. The hunter cannot simply choose the weirdest moving body because a human friend may be intentionally acting suspicious or accidentally panicking.
Humans can bait the hunter to protect themselves or to troll friends.
Humans can accidentally look more alien than the alien.
Humans keep rounds funny even when the NPC AI is simple.
Humans reduce the need for a large or perfect crowd simulation.

8.1 Movement Feel
Civilian bodies accelerate gradually rather than launching instantly.
Releasing movement causes a short coast instead of an immediate stop.
Opposite input performs intentional braking.
Sharp turns create a readable correction step.
Objects and NPCs punish sloppy movement through mild bump reactions, not heavy penalties.
Good players look smooth. Bad players look like they are piloting a body for the first time.
8.2 Act Natural
Act Natural is the main player-owned recovery mechanic. It should sometimes save the player and sometimes make them look even more suspicious. The same button exists for Humans and the Alien.

9.2 Hunter Fun Features
The hunter triggers two public commands to create crowd reactions.
The hunter asks one direct question to a chosen suspect.
The hunter can mark suspects to keep track of doubt.
The hunter can bluff with a fake accusation if included late in MVP.
The hunter has one real accusation, making the final decision dramatic.
The hunter also has to remain socially believable enough that civilians do not scatter or derail the round.
9.3 Undercover Hunter Soft Rules
Do not build a big hunter suspicion meter for MVP. Instead, use soft reactions.
If hunter sprints or shoves, nearby NPCs look uncomfortable and step away.
If hunter stares too closely for too long, humans can notice and become nervous.
If hunter repeatedly harasses people, public barks shame them and create comedy.
A wrong accusation should produce a strong crowd reaction and funny reveal.
10. Alien Gameplay
The Alien is not a monster visually. The Alien is secretly driving a human body. The fun is not having powers; the fun is surviving social pressure while acting just normal enough.
10.1 Alien Goals
Do not get identified by the hunter.
React to public commands in a believable way.
Do not overperform normality.
Use Act Natural to recover from mistakes.
Use human players and weird NPCs as cover.
Win by surviving the single accusation.
10.2 Alien Memory Moments
The Alien remains calm while a Human player panics and gets accused.
The Alien makes a weird mistake, presses Act Natural, and somehow sells the lie.
The hunter questions the Alien and the Alien chooses the most normal answer while holding coffee upside down.
The Alien stands next to a suspicious civilian to frame them.
The Alien survives because the Hunter overthinks a harmless NPC quirk.
10.3 Alien Restrictions
No hard invisibility.
No teleporting.
No disabling the hunter.
No combat.
No complex task list in MVP.
No disguise durability system in MVP.
11. Human Gameplay
Human players are the chaos engine. They are not passive civilians. They create false positives, protect themselves, bait the hunter, and generate funny social noise.
11.1 Human Goals
Avoid being falsely accused.
React to public commands like a normal person.
Use Act Natural and props just like the Alien can.
Optionally bait suspicion in private lobbies if the group enjoys trolling.
In public lobbies, score incentives should reward not being falsely accused and not griefing the round.
11.2 Human Player Micro-Objectives
Humans do not need mandatory task lists. To keep them engaged, give them tiny optional errands that look like normal behaviour. These should be bonus score only, not required to win.
Optional Human Errand
Why It Helps
Buy one item
Gives a human a reason to move, stand in line, and look suspicious.
Find a mirror and inspect outfit
Creates motion and false positives.
Carry coffee without spilling
Creates physical comedy.
Respond correctly to two commands
Teaches the social reaction rules.
Do not get accused
Keeps humans invested in self-preservation.
11.3 Human Trolling Boundaries
Private lobbies can lean into chaotic trolling.
Public lobbies need soft scoring and cooldowns to prevent humans from ruining every round.
Humans should be allowed to act suspicious, but not spam disruptive actions indefinitely.
The game should celebrate funny wrong accusations without encouraging harassment.

17. UE5.8 Technical Architecture
This section is designed so the project can be started in C++ with Blueprint-friendly hooks. Enhanced Input is recommended for role-specific mapping contexts, StateTree for simple NPC logic, Smart Objects for reusable store interactions, and normal Unreal replication for listen-server multiplayer. Epic describes StateTree as a hierarchical state machine that combines behavior-tree selectors with state-machine transitions, and Smart Objects as level objects that provide interaction data for AI agents and players.

Do not hard-code command names into NPC logic.

Do not hard-code role reveal copy into GameMode.
Use Gameplay Tags and Data Assets so content can be changed without recompiling.
Keep the first data set tiny. Two commands, three props, six quirks is enough.

20.1 Animation Best Practices
Use the same animation baseline for Human players, Alien player, and NPCs.
Do not give the Alien unique locomotion animations that make them easy to spot.
Use slight timing and posture differences as player-driven output, not fixed alien tells.
Prioritize clean start/stop/turn animations before adding extra emotes.
For early prototype, use mannequins or Manny/Quinn-compatible animations before custom art.

24. MVP Scope Lock
24.1 Required MVP
Minimum 3 players: 1 Hunter, 1 Alien, 1 Human.
Public/private lobby creation and join flow in basic form.
Lobby level with physical host Play button.
Server-authoritative random role assignment.
Private role reveal screen.
One compact clothing store level.
4-8 NPCs with simple StateTree routines.
Civilian shared controls for Human and Alien.
Hunter first-person controls.
Two Public Commands: Freeze and Look Here.

Multiple store zones or mall map.
More than one Alien.

Built-in VOIP requirement.

Large MetaHuman crowds.
